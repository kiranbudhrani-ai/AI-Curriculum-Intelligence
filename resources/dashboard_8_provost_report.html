Section 1

Executive Summary

A catalog-wide scan of the 2026 undergraduate and graduate course records finds that AI-related curriculum activity has moved decisively beyond its historical base in Computing & Informatics, with 2026 marking the clearest inflection point in the data to date.

  • 01AI curriculum activity is expanding beyond Computing & Informatics. AI signals now appear in 26 disciplines across 6 of 8 colleges, up from a historically concentrated technical footprint.
  • 022026 is the inflection point for AI-signal growth. Of 89 dated AI-signal records, 46 became effective in 2026 alone — 51.7% — more than every prior year combined back to 2017.
  • 03Tier 1 and Tier 2 signals tell different stories. Tier 1 records carry AI directly in the course title (67 records); Tier 2 records carry AI only in the course description (50 records) — activity that a title-only review would miss entirely.
  • 04Colleges are at different adoption stages. Computing & Informatics, Humanities/Earth & Social Sciences, and Business are accelerating; Engineering is expanding; Education and Arts + Architecture are emerging; Science and Health & Human Services show no signal yet.
  • 05These are catalog-level signals, not measures of enrollment, seat capacity, or instructional depth. The findings show where AI is visible in the catalog — not how many students take these courses or how deeply AI is taught within them.

Section 7

Implications for Academic Planning

The data points to a curriculum in transition: AI is moving from a specialized technical footprint toward a broader institutional presence, with 2026 representing a clear step-change rather than gradual drift.

Curriculum Planning

2026's concentration of new AI-signal records suggests this is an active window for coordinated curriculum mapping, before AI content becomes distributed unevenly across colleges without central visibility.

AI Literacy

With 50 of 117 records (Tier 2) embedding AI only in descriptions, students and advisors relying on titles alone may under-recognize where AI already appears in their coursework.

Faculty Development

Accelerating colleges (Computing, Business, Humanities/Earth & Social Sciences) may benefit from shared instructional resources, while emerging colleges may need different, earlier-stage support.

Policy Alignment

The academic integrity cross-check shows AI-related case activity extending well beyond catalog-labeled AI courses, suggesting institution-wide policy guidance may be more relevant than course-specific rules alone.

Cross-College Coordination

Uneven adoption stages across colleges point toward an opportunity for a cross-college working group to share emerging practice before parallel, disconnected efforts take root.

Data & Measurement

These findings are catalog-level signals only. Enrollment, seat capacity, and instructional-depth data would meaningfully extend this picture for future planning cycles.
