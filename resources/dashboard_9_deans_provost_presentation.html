01 · Why this analysis now?

The leadership question

How quickly is AI entering the curriculum, where is that growth occurring, and what institutional coordination may be needed as AI moves across disciplines?

Individual curriculum proposals show pieces of the change. This analysis creates an institution-wide view of the pattern.
Facilitator cue: Establish that this is a directional planning view, not an evaluation of colleges or programs.
02 · How to read the evidence

Three different catalog conditions

The analysis detects formal AI visibility in course titles and descriptions. It does not claim that catalog language captures all AI teaching activity.

Tier 1 · Visible AI

AI appears in the course title

Clearly visible to students, advisors, faculty, and academic leaders.

Tier 2 · Embedded AI

AI appears in the description only

Substantive catalog activity that a title-only review would miss.

No catalog signal

AI is absent from title and description

This does not necessarily mean AI is absent from instruction or faculty practice.

Facilitator cue: Pause here. The distinction prevents “no signal” from being interpreted as “no activity.”

10 · AI extends beyond AI-labeled courses

The catalog captures formal adoption—not the full institutional effect

65

Distinct courses with AI-tagged academic integrity cases across 2024–2026.

Only 2 overlapped with the pre-2026 AI-signal catalog.

The other 63 courses were not catalog-labeled as AI. This does not imply risk in AI courses; it shows that student AI use and related policy questions are occurring broadly across the curriculum.

Institution-wide guidance may be more relevant than rules limited to formally AI-labeled courses.
11 · What the evidence suggests

A curriculum in transition

Institution-wide, not single-collegeAI is moving from a specialized technical footprint toward broad disciplinary presence.
Faster than normal visibility systemsThe 2026 step-change is difficult to see through isolated proposals and title-only scans.
Different colleges need different supportAccelerating, expanding, and emerging contexts should not receive a one-size-fits-all response.
Catalog data is necessary but incompleteEnrollment, capacity, demand, learning outcomes, and instructional depth remain outside this scan.
12 · Implications for academic planning

Six areas for coordinated attention

01

Curriculum Mapping

Create a recurring institution-wide view before activity becomes fragmented or difficult to coordinate.

02

Student Visibility

Help students and advisors see embedded AI learning that is not apparent from titles.

03

Faculty Development

Differentiate support for colleges that are accelerating, expanding, or just emerging.

04

Policy Alignment

Use institution-wide expectations because AI use extends beyond AI-labeled courses.

05

Cross-College Coordination

Surface complementary pathways and reduce disconnected or duplicative development.

06

Data & Measurement

Add enrollment, seat capacity, demand, and instructional-depth indicators in future cycles.

Questions for the Provost and Deans Council

Student accessWhich students and programs currently have meaningful access to AI learning?

SupportWhat differentiated support do accelerating, expanding, and emerging colleges need?

Proposed next phase

Develop an annual AI Curriculum Intelligence review combining catalog signals, enrollment, seat capacity, instructional depth, program mapping, and college-level validation.

14 · Close

The decision is not whether AI is entering the curriculum.

The evidence shows that it already is. The leadership opportunity is to make the growth visible, coherent, equitable, and academically meaningful.
